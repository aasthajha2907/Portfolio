The Origin
On the record

She made history class
worth reading.

Long before she was building brands, she was building audiences — starting with her 8th standard class and a comic book nobody asked for.

In 8th standard, I turned an entire history syllabus into a comic book because textbooks bored me. My class used it to study. They scored the highest average that semester. I was thirteen, and I had accidentally figured out the thing that would define my entire career: if you understand your audience deeply enough, you can make anything compelling.

"If you understand your audience deeply enough, you can make anything compelling."

That is still how I work, twenty years later — except now the audience is your customer, and the textbook is your product.

Before I write a single word, I sit with the people who built it. Engineers, founders, product managers — I want to know every feature, every edge case, every detail that didn't make the pitch deck. Because the thing that makes customers fall in love with a product is almost never the headline USP. It is something quieter. More specific. Usually sitting in a Notion doc that nobody thought to show the marketing team.

My job is to find it. Name it. And build a brand around it that people actually remember.

My writing adapts to every brand I work with — I wear the tone, not the other way around. Every project gets obsessive research, sharp strategy, and the kind of preparation that makes "winging it" physically impossible for me.

I work best with brands that have a reason to exist beyond the bottom line. When I'm not in a strategy doc, I'm 16 hours into my screen time, overwatering my plants, and on my third coffee. Introvert. Chronically online. Occasionally found talking to a pothos.

DECISION FATIGUE
Hypothetical campaign concept — Swiggy

Swiggy isn't
a food app.
It's a decision
outsourcing engine.

Nobody is advertising the 7 minutes before the order. That's the gap.

The real insight

Everyone in this category is screaming fast delivery, hot food, best price. Meanwhile the actual user just stared at their fridge for 7 minutes and closed it. People don't open Swiggy because they're hungry. They open it because they're burnt out, had a bad day, need a win, or just want something good to happen. Market the mood. Not the meal.


Zomato is loud, witty, the brand that talks at you. Swiggy could own something quieter — the app that feels like it was made specifically for your worst Tuesdays. That's a position nobody can take away once you own it.

Headline options

— For when deciding is harder than eating.
02 You don't need a plan. Just open Swiggy.
03 We already knew. You just had to open the app.
04 Brain offline. Swiggy online.
05 You kept it together all day. The evening owes you.
01
The Fridge Moment
Advertise the 7 minutes before the order. The scroll, the indecision, the "ugh fine." That's the real trigger and nobody's touching it.
02
Mood not Hunger
Tired. Bad day. Need a win. Main character night. Every emotional state maps to a meal — and Swiggy already knows which one.
03
You Survived Today
Not "treat yourself" — that's tired. More like: "You kept it together all day. The least the evening can do is not involve cooking."
04
It Already Knows
Swiggy knows your comfort food, your 11pm patterns, your bad decisions. Lean into it — not creepy, just accurate.
