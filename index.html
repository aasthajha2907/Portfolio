About Me

I didn't choose writing. Writing chose me — loudly, and early.

In 8th standard, I turned an entire history syllabus into a comic book because textbooks bored me. My class used it to study. They scored the highest average that semester. I was thirteen, and I had accidentally figured out the thing that would define my entire career: if you understand your audience deeply enough, you can make anything compelling.

That is still how I work, twenty years later — except now the audience is your customer, and the textbook is your product.

Before I write a single word, I sit with the people who built it. Engineers, founders, product managers — I want to know every feature, every edge case, every detail that didn't make the pitch deck. Not because I need to write a manual. Because the thing that makes customers fall in love with a product is almost never the headline USP. It is something quieter. More specific. Usually sitting in a Notion doc that nobody thought to show the marketing team.

My job is to find it. Name it. And build a brand around it that people actually remember.

My writing adapts to every brand I work with — I wear the tone, not the other way around. Every project gets obsessive research, sharp strategy, and the kind of preparation that makes "winging it" physically impossible for me.

I work best with brands that have a reason to exist beyond the bottom line — the kind building something for the next generation, not just the next quarter.

When I'm not in a strategy doc, I'm 16 hours into my screen time, overwatering my plants, and on my third coffee. Introvert. Chronically online. Occasionally found talking to a pothos.

Campaign Concept

Swiggy — "I Give Up, Just Handle It"

A hypothetical campaign concept exploring the under-leveraged psychological insight behind why people actually open food delivery apps.

The insight
Swiggy isn't a food delivery app.
It's a decision outsourcing engine.

Every competitor is screaming fast delivery, hot food, best price. Meanwhile the actual user just stared at their fridge for 7 minutes and closed it. Nobody is advertising that moment. That's the gap.

What the category keeps saying
30-minute delivery
1000+ restaurants
Best deals, best prices
Your favourite food, faster
What's actually happening on the user's end
Opened the fridge. Closed it. Opened it again.
Scrolled 5 restaurants. Ordered nothing. Closed the app.
Just got off a 9-hour shift. Brain is done.
Ordered the same thing as last Tuesday. Again.
4 places the campaign lives
01. The fridge moment
Forget the order. Advertise the 7 minutes before it. The scroll, the indecision, the "ugh fine." That's the real Swiggy trigger and nobody's touching it.
02. Mood, not hunger
People don't open Swiggy because they're hungry. They open it because they're tired, had a bad day, need a win, or just want something good to happen. Market the mood. Not the meal.
03. You survived today
Not "treat yourself" — that's been done to death. More like: "You kept it together all day. The least the evening can do is not involve cooking." Low-key permission slip energy.
04. It already knows
Swiggy knows your comfort food, your 11pm patterns, your Tuesday slumps. That's kind of intimate. Lean into it — not in a creepy way, in a "this app gets me" way that Zomato can't claim.
Headlines
For when deciding is harder than eating.
You don't need a plan. Just open Swiggy.
We already knew. You just had to open the app.
Brain offline. Swiggy online.
Why this works against Zomato
Zomato is
Loud. Witty. The brand that talks at you. Great for awareness, not great for the quiet 11pm scroll.
Swiggy could be
The one that gets it without saying much. Personal. A little too accurate. The app that feels like it was made specifically for your worst Tuesdays.
